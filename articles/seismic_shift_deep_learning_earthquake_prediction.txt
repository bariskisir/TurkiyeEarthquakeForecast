Abstract. Earthquakes are a big threat. They kill people, wreck buildings, and 
mess up economies. We need to predict them well to lower risks and handle dis-
asters better. This project uses deep learning methods, such as neural networks 
and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models, to guess earthquake sizes and 
depths. It looks at seismic data from the USGS Earthquake Database. The team 
made and tested several models checking their Mean Squared Error (MSE) and 
Mean Absolute Error (MAE). The final output demonstrates that the LSTM 
model does the best job. It catches time-based patterns and makes good guesses. 
There are still problems to solve, but this way of doing things looks promising 
for earthquake prediction. It shows we need to keep studying to make our guesses 
more accurate and reliable. 
Keywords: Earthquakes, Deep Learning, Neural Networks, LSTM, Earthquake 
Prediction, Seismic Data, Disaster Management. 
1 
Introduction 
Earthquakes are a severe risk factor to people, to buildings and economies, worldwide. 
Due to the unpredictability of where and when they occur, it is very challenging to 
hypothesize when and at what location they will occur, making it extremely challenging 
to get ready in advance. The ability to forecast size (magnitude) and the extent of an 
earthquake (depth) could be used to save lives, improve evacuation plans, and manage 
resources in a more efficient way. Thanks to the new developments in machine learning 
and deep learning, we are now able to try to predict earthquakes more accurately ana-
lyzing seismic data in a more intelligent way. In the past, earthquake forecasting pri-
marily used to be based on research of characteristics such as breaks, plates, and past 
earthquake records. 
Although these approaches have been successful, they are not always very accurate due 
to the complexity and unpredictability of earthquakes. The Deep learning architectures, 
e.g., neural networks and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models, have been pro-
posed as one solution. These models can ingest and generate massive amounts of data 
and identify patterns in data that it is difficult to see for human eyes. This project ex-
plores how deep learning can help predict earthquake magnitudes and depths using data

indicates that LSTM networks are especially suitable to estimate earthquake data. As a 
conclusion, this work demonstrates that deep learning, particularly using LSTM, is a 
promising tool to predict earthquake magnitudes and depths. The findings also high-
light the importance of selecting the proper model, optimizing its parameters, and ap-
plying regularization methods. Although the LSTM achieved the top results, there is 
still room for improvement by incorporating more fine-tuning and preprocessing data. 
These results have the potential for more advanced earthquake prediction systems that 
can be used to help improve disaster preparedness and attenuation of earthquake related 
damage.

Proposed System 
The developed system is based around earthquake magnitude and depth prediction us-
ing state-of-the-art deep (i.e., neural network and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) 
based) models. The goal is to develop a system that can provide precise predictions by 
modeling the relevant variables, i.e., geographic coordinates (longitude, latitude) and 
seismic depth. Using these variables, the system intends to give useful information 
about earthquake intensity and depth, both extremely valuable for earthquakes forecast-
ing and for disaster management.  
The heart of the suggested system is based on the data preprocessing, which is the scal-
ing, normalization and data split for training, validation and test sets. This helps that 
the model can generalize sufficiently to a new data. In this system, a variety of deep 
learning models are employed to predict earthquake magnitudes and depths. The model 
is a feedforward neural network model, with 3 dense layers as architecture. This model 
is trained through the Adam optimizer and further elevated by a grid search to the dis-
covery of good hyperparameter settings (i.e., batch size and epochs).  
Due to the establishment of the proposed system, the second neural network model is 
established by using the architecture same as those of the first one, but it is wrapped 
inside KerasRegressor wrapper with scikit-learn’s GridSearchCV for hyperparameter 
fine motor control. Besides, the RMSprop optimizer is employed to train this model, 
and therefore enhancing its prediction performance is accomplished. The third model 
has the dropout regularization with larger number of neurons in the hidden layers with 
a view to facilitate learning capacity and gain against overfitting. Dropout helps en-
hance the generalisation of a model by randomly deactivating a portion of neurons dur-
ing training.  
In the concept of time-series prediction, the suggested system uses Long Short-Term 
Memory (LSTM) model. LSTMs have the advantage that sequential data can be treated 
well and in this instance, it is possible to learn temporal dependencies in earthquake 
data. LSTM model consists of 2 such layers with each 64 units deep, and a dropout 
layer to avoid overfitting. The system uses Adam optimizer with mean squared error 
(MSE) loss function and early stopping to avoid overfitting during training. 
In order to judge the proposed system, Mean squared error (MSE) and Mean absolute 
error (MAE) are calculated in the validation and test phase. These measures facilitate 
modeling of the performance tradeoffs between models to characterize the tradeoff in 
performance between models and determine how each model lends itself better for es-
timating the magnitudes and the depths of earthquakes.

Preprocessing 
The earthquake prediction project centers around the preprocessing and data augmen-
tation techniques to increase the model’s ability to be generalized to new, unknown 
data. Augmentation of data is concerned with image datasets but preprocessing is very 
important when dealing with time series data (e.g., earthquake magnitude and depth). 
A short description of typical preprocessing steps and transformations is provided: 
 Normalization (Rescaling): The project scales features such as magnitude and depth 
to a consistent range of [0 1]. This scaling ensures the model gives equal weight to all 
input features and helps the gradient descent process converge more, which speeds up 
the training.  
Time-Based Feature Creation: In the project, the matching Date and Time columns 
are merged into the single Timestamp Column to enable the model to recognize and 
capture temporal patterns. This is also relevant for forecasting the magnitudes and 
depths of earthquakes since cyclic patterns in seismic activity may be found, based on 
time.  
Dealing with Missing Data: We deal with the dataset hole by imputation/deleting 
missing values. This makes sure the dataset is complete. With this methodology, we 
eliminate the training of errors and guarantee that, during training, we actually use con-
sistent reliable data. 
Outlier Detection:We identify extreme values in important features such as magnitude 
and depth by means of stats-based approaches, i.e., the Z-score. Eliminating outliers 
allows the model to focus more on the prototypical patterns in the data and avoid being 
mislead by extreme, non-representative values. 
Noise Reduction: We smooth or eliminate noise in the data, which allows the model 
to concentrate on salient patterns rather than spurious ups and downs. Hence the model 
becomes not only more effective but also easier to be understood.  
Training-Testing and Split: Here splitting the data into training and testing sets to 
evaluate the model on data it has not seen before. At this stage it is decisive for evalu-
ating the extent to which the model will be able to generalize what it has learned to 
situations which will be encountered in practice.

Methodology 
In this work, a range of neural network architectures and an LSTM model are investi-
gated for earthquake magnitude and depth prediction. Each model was systematically 
designed, trained, and evaluated to capture complex patterns within the data while en-
suring high prediction accuracy and robustness. 
5.1 
Neural Network Model 1 
Feedforward Network The feedforward neural network model is built as the 3 dense 
layers. The architecture is constituted by an input layer (which has the number of two 
neuron’s recording magnitude and depth) and two hidden layers (with 16 neurons) and 
an output layer (whose number of neurons for the prediction is 2). ReLU type activation 
is applied to input the non-linearity to hidden layers, and the softmax activation function 
is deployed for output layer to classify tasks. 
 
Fig. 1.Architecture Neural Network 1 
Grid search optimization determined the optimal batch size of 32 and 20 epochs for 
training. Adam optimizer trained on the MSE to a final MSE of-97.94 on the validation 
set, which reflects the model capacity to represent relations among the input features 
and the corresponding outputs. 
5.2
Neural Network Model 2
 
GridSearch-Optimized Network, in the second neural network the KerasRegressor 
wrapper and scikit-learn’s GridSearchCV are combined iteratively to fine-tune learning 
of hyperparameters. Its architecture is very similar to the previous one and consists of 
an input layer of 2 neurons, of two hidden layers of 16 units and an output layer of 2 
neurons. The “ReLU” activation can be seen on the hidden layers and the RMSprop is 
used as the outcome optimizer.

Conclusion 
This project effectively utilized deep learning techniques, including neural networks 
and LSTM models, to predict earthquake magnitudes and depths. The research found 
that deep learning (especially LSTM models) outperformed regular methods because 
the former could learn intricate non-lineres relationships a mong seismic data. 
 As the LSTM model is capable of learning the temporal dependence, the LSTM model 
outperformed the other models in terms of magnitude and depth with low Mean Abso-
lute Error (MAE) and Mean Squared Error (MSE) for its predictions. Such as it is rea-
sonable to choose models that can handle the sequential nature inherent property of 
earthquake time series. Despite of its success, model performance is still potentially 
enhanced by optimization, such as by improving feature engineering, using a wider 
application of complementary input variables (i.e., models about the tectonic defor-
mation of continents and plates), as well as the strength of the model architecture.
