Abstract
Machine Learning techniques have gained traction in earthquake forecasting, with recent studies reporting exceptionally 
high accuracy rates. However, concerns regarding the reliability of these models in real-world scenarios persist. This study 
provides a critical reappraisal of a recently proposed Machine Learning workflow that originally reported >97% accuracy 
in forecasting seismic events. We replicated the methodology using a new dataset from Tokyo, Japan, to assess its gener¬≠
alizability and robustness. While standard random train-test splits reproduced the originally reported high accuracy (>99%) 
in this distinct tectonic setting, subsequent analysis revealed this performance to be an artifact of data leakage. When sub¬≠
jected to rigorous time-based validation and walk-forward testing, the model‚Äôs predictive performance dropped drastically 
to near-baseline levels (24%). Furthermore, direct cross-location testing yielded results indistinguishable from random 
chance (16%), confirming that the model relies on local artifacts rather than physical precursors. Our findings suggest that 
the promising results often found in the literature may stem from improper handling of seismic data characteristics. We 
conclude that the advance of ML-based earthquake forecasting requires a fundamental shift towards stricter methodologi¬≠
cal standards, prioritizing realistic validation protocols and domain-specific rigor over optimistic performance metrics.

Background
Seismic events represent one of the natural disasters with the 
greatest socioeconomic impact worldwide. The economic 
consequences are substantial. For example, a 2023 report 
from the US Geological Survey (USGS) estimated that 
earthquakes cost the United States an annual $14.7 billion 
in building damage and related losses alone (Jaiswal 2023). 
Regarding the impact in human lives, the figures are equally 
alarming. The World Health Organization (WHO) reported 
that in the 1998-2017 period, over 125 million people were 
affected by earthquakes, resulting in injury, displacement, or 
loss of their homes (Organization 2025). Furthermore, spe¬≠
cific tools exist for quantifying and forecasting the human 
impact following a seismic event (Wang et¬Ýal. 2024). Thus, 
seismic forecasting is established as a priority research field. 
Although predictive strategies must operate on a local scale 
due to the high dependence on the seismotectonic charac¬≠
teristics of each region, the precise estimation of a future 
earthquake‚Äôs magnitude is a crucial factor. This estimation 
is fundamental for the design of effective mitigation plans 
that allow for the reduction of the devastating social and 
economic impact of these phenomena (Aksoy et¬Ýal. 2024).
The preceding evidence underscores the need to develop 
seismic forecasting methods that encompass not only the 
magnitude, but also the location, time of occurrence, and 
its probability. However, the intrinsically chaotic and non-
linear nature of seismogenic processes represents a formi¬≠
dable scientific challenge, often requiring adaptive signal 
processing techniques to handle non-stationary data (Singh 
and Roy 2025; Mache et¬Ýal. 2022). Despite these difficul¬≠
ties, the scientific community has produced an extensive 
amount of work in this area, proposing a diverse range of 
methodologies (Tehseen et¬Ýal. 2020; Mousavi and Beroza 
2023; Chitkeshwar 2024).
Existing approaches can be broadly classified into two main 
categories: those based on Machine Learning models applied 
to catalog data and those that employ time-series analysis of 
continuous precursor signals. The following section describes 
some of the most representative works in these areas.
A notable study is the work of Wang et¬Ýal. (2022), who 
approaches the problem from the perspective of both time-
series and non-time-series data. The aim of their work is 
to create a system for monitoring and collecting anomalous 
signals that act as earthquake precursors. Using this data, 
the authors evaluate and compare classic predictive models 
to determine the most suitable one. Their results conclude 
that the LSTM (Long-Short Term Memory) model provides 
the highest performance in their experimental framework.
Similarly, Saad et¬Ýal. (2023) proposed a framework based 
on electromagnetic and geo-acoustic data from the AETA 
network. Unlike typical retrospective analyses, they con¬≠
ducted a 30-week real-time forecasting test, achieving an 
accuracy of 70%. This approach highlights the potential of 
integrating physical precursor signals with Machine Learn¬≠
ing models under prospective evaluation conditions
Within the machine learning-based approaches, a rep¬≠
resentative work is the work of Singh and Roy (2025), 
focused on forecasting the magnitude of earthquakes in 
India. The distinctive contribution of this study is the use 
of geographical and meteorological data for the construc¬≠
tion of the input variables (features). The authors evaluate 
and compare the performance of several algorithms, specifi¬≠
cally Gradient Boosting, Light Gradient Boosting Machine 
(LightGBM), XGBoost, and Random Forest. Their findings 
reveal that the optimal model varies according to the size of 
the dataset: Gradient Boosting is more effective for small

Based on the review of the state-of-the-art, and in light 
of the outstanding performance reported by Yavas et¬Ý al. 
(2024), the present work focuses on a systematic evaluation 
of this methodology. The objective is to analyze its robust¬≠

methodological validity regarding temporal causality.
To guide this research, the following hypotheses are 
proposed:
Hypothesis 1‚ÄÇ (Generalizability) The methodology pre¬≠
sented can be extended to other locations and maintain its 
predictive capabilities. To corroborate this hypothesis, the 
same experiment presented for Los Angeles has been rep¬≠
licated for the city of Tokyo, Japan. Given that Tokyo pres¬≠
ents a fundamentally different seismotectonic environment, 
dominated by subduction zones compared to the strike-slip 
regime of Los Angeles, this test evaluates whether the model 
learns universal seismic precursors or site-specific artifacts.
Hypothesis 2‚ÄÇ (Temporal Causality) The employed method¬≠
ology respects the temporal structure of seismic time-series 
during the training and validation process. To corroborate 
this hypothesis, a rigorous ‚Äúwalk-forward‚Äù validation test 
will be performed. Seismic events are causally linked (e.g., 
mainshock-aftershock sequences following Omori‚Äôs Law Utsu 
1961), meaning that the order in which the data is divided must 
be preserved. If the original methodology failed to account for 
this causality (inducing data leakage Kapoor and Narayanan 
2022), the results under strict temporal validation should drop 
significantly compared to the results presented by the authors.
The empirical validation of these hypotheses constitutes 
the fundamental contribution of this work, shedding light on 
the real-world applicability and limitations of the evaluated 
methodology for seismic forecasting.
Experimental replication
As previously mentioned, this work focuses on the critical 
replication of the study by Yavas et¬Ýal. (2024). In their work, 
the authors aim to forecast the largest magnitude event in 
the next 30 days, framing it as a multi-class classification 
problem. Their study focuses on the Los Angeles area, a 
densely populated region characterized by a transform plate 
boundary system involving the Pacific and North Ameri¬≠
can plates, hosting major active faults such as San Andreas, 
Puente Hills, and Santa Monica. This combination of high 
population density and active faulting renders Los Angeles 
one of the highest seismic risk urban areas globally.
In the original work, authors utilized the earthquake cata¬≠
log provided by the Southern California Earthquake Data 
Center (SCEDC), selecting events occurring within a 100 
km radius of central Los Angeles from January 2012 to Sep¬≠
tember 2024. To ensure consistency, all magnitudes were 
converted to a unified scale using empirical relationships. 
The dataset comprises over 23,000 events, whose magni¬≠
tude range varies between 0.65 and 5.5 once converted.
The remarkable performance reported by Yavas et¬Ýal. 
(2024), with metrics summarized in Table 1, motivates the 
testing of their approach in other high-seismicity regions. 
To evaluate the generalizability of this methodological 
framework, this study extends its application to a distinct 
tectonic setting: Tokyo, Japan. Although Los Angeles is 
situated on a transform boundary, Tokyo is located near 
a complex triple junction dominated by subduction pro¬≠
cesses. We intentionally apply the original study‚Äôs spa¬≠
tial criterion (a 100 km radius around the city center) to 
this new environment. This allows us to assess whether 
the claimed predictive capability is robust across different 
seismotectonic regimes or if it is an artifact specific to the 
original dataset constraints.
The procedure defined by the original authors has been

1.	 Train the model using historical data available up to 
time t.
2.	 Evaluate the forecasting performance on a future win¬≠
dow from t + 1 to t + n, where n is the length of the 
evaluation period.
3.	 Advance the time window (roll forward), incorporating 
the observed data into the training set.
4.	 Repeat the process across the entire timeline.
The final performance is calculated as the aggregate of all 
iterations, providing a realistic estimate of how the model 
would perform in a live monitoring scenario.

Discussion
The results of this study provide conclusive evidence that 
the exceptional performance reported in recent Machine 
Learning-based earthquake forecasting literature is, to a 
significant extent, an artifact of data-leakage arising from 
validation protocols that ignore the causal structure of seis¬≠
mic data. By strictly replicating a representative high-per¬≠
forming workflow, authors demonstrated that the reported 
accuracy (>99%) is not a measure of predictive skill, but 
rather a quantification of the ability to interpolate between 
known events, using future information that would not be 
available in a real-time operation‚Äìa phenomenon effectively 
constituting overfitting.
The breakdown of this performance under rigorous test¬≠
ing reveals the precise nature of the failure. First, the Cross-
Location Generalization Assessment demonstrated that 
models trained in one tectonic setting fail catastrophically 
when applied to another (accuracy dropping to ¬Ý16%). If 
the models were truly learning universal physical precur¬≠
sors of seismicity, some degree of transferability would be 
expected. The fact that they perform "perfectly" in their 
native training environment but fail purely at random in a 
new location indicates that the models are not learning seis¬≠
mology, but are instead overfitting to the specific statistical 
idiosyncrasies and noise of their local catalog.
Secondly, we addressed the concern that data leakage 
might stem from specific input features, such as rolling 
b-values or magnitudes, which can encode future informa¬≠
tion if defined loosely. However, our ablation study revealed 
that even after removing all features potentially susceptible 
to this leakage (e.g., b-values, rolling means), the model 
trained with a random split still achieved >97% accuracy 
on Tokyo dataset. This confirms that the leakage is not pri¬≠
marily feature-driven but methodology-driven. The ran¬≠
dom splitting strategy creates a training set rich in samples 
temporally adjacent to the test samples. Given the high 
autocorrelation of seismic time-series, the model learns to 
interpolate these neighbors rather than forecast future states.
This conclusion is solidified by the Temporally Indepen¬≠
dent Hold-out evaluation. The stark contrast between the 
near-perfect accuracy (>99%) on the randomized test set 
and the baseline performance (29%) on the strictly separated 
future hold-out set serves as a definitive proof of look-ahead 
bias. The model effectively operates as a sophisticated inter¬≠
polator within the known timeline but loses all utility when 
forced to extrapolate to unseen future data.
This study reinforces the warning that increased algo¬≠
rithmic complexity cannot compensate for a lack of 
rigorous experimental design. When strict temporal cau¬≠
sality was enforced, the model‚Äôs performance collapsed to 
The results of this experiment illustrate a stark contrast 
in performance depending on the evaluation scheme. When 
evaluated on the randomized test set, the model reproduced 
the originally reported metrics, achieving an accuracy 
exceeding 0.99. However, when the exact same model was 
evaluated on the chronologically reserved hold-out set, the 
performance collapsed drastically.
The model achieved an accuracy of 0.29, with a weighted 
precision of 0.46, a recall of 0.29, and an F1-score of 0.27. 
These results are consistent with the baseline levels observed 
in the walk-forward validation. This discrepancy confirms 
that the model‚Äôs apparent success on the random split is due 
to its ability to interpolate within the time series using future 
information (leakage), rather than a genuine capability to 
extrapolate and forecast future seismic events.
Cross-location generalization assessment
To definitively assess whether the models have learned 
universal seismic precursors or merely memorized local 
statistical structures (Hypothesis 1), a direct cross-location 
generalization test was performed. In this experiment, the 
model trained on the Los Angeles dataset was directly eval¬≠
uated on the Tokyo dataset without any retraining or fine-
tuning, and vice versa.
A methodological adjustment was necessary to ensure 
compatibility between the distinct datasets. Since the Los 
Angeles dataset provided by the original authors contained 
only the discrete target class labels (and not the raw con¬≠
tinuous magnitude of the target event), it was not possible 
to recalculate its classes to match the Tokyo distribution. 
Therefore, we aligned the Tokyo dataset‚Äôs target classes to 
strictly match the class boundaries and definitions used in 
the Los Angeles model.
The results of this cross-evaluation demonstrate a 
complete collapse in the predictive capability of the 
specific models generated by this workflow. The model 
trained on Los Angeles data, which achieved an accu¬≠
racy of 0.9797 in its native environment, yielded an 
accuracy of 0.16, precision of 0.12, recall of 0.16, and 
F1-score of 0.08 when applied to Tokyo. Conversely, the 
model trained on Tokyo data, with a native accuracy of 
>0.99, dropped to an accuracy of 0.15, precision of 0.15, 
recall of 0.15, and F1-score of 0.12 when tested on Los 
Angeles data.
These performance metrics, which fall below the level 
of random chance for a six-class problem, strongly indicate 
that the high accuracy reported in the original study is not 
derived from transferable physical features of seismogene¬≠
sis, but rather from overfitting to site-specific characteristics 
and local data artifacts.

2.	 Information-Based Evaluation: Accuracy is often a mis¬≠
leading metric in imbalanced datasets like earthquake 
catalogs. CSEP utilizes likelihood-based tests (e.g., 
N-test, L-test) that penalize both missed events and 
false alarms more robustly than simple classification

Furthermore, any ML model claiming utility must be 
benchmarked against operational baselines, most notably 
the Epidemic-Type Aftershock Sequence (ETAS) model. 
Since ETAS explicitly captures the temporal clustering and 
triggering that ML models often exploit implicitly (or erro¬≠
neously via leakage), it remains the de-facto standard for 
short-term seismicity forecasting. Demonstrating consistent 
outperformance over ETAS within a CSEP-style framework 
should be considered a minimum criterion before claiming 
practical utility.
Beyond strict validation protocols, there is a need to 
reconsider the forecasting target itself. The framing of 
earthquake forecasting as a simple classification task (e.g., 
predicting "maximum magnitude" bins), as seen in the rep¬≠
licated study, often oversimplifies the seismogenic process. 
Operational forecasting benefits significantly from esti¬≠
mating tangible physical parameters, such as stress field 
variations and pressure changes, rather than relying solely 
on discrete magnitude labels (Kroll and Sherman 2023). 
Although metrics like log-loss can improve probabilistic 
interpretation, future ML efforts should aim to integrate 
with physical constraints, modeling these continuous state 
parameters to directly inform hazard assessment.
In conclusion, while Machine Learning offers powerful 
tools for seismology, its application to forecasting requires 
a fundamental shift towards stricter methodological stan¬≠
dards. Our analysis shows that ignoring the temporal nature 
of seismic phenomena leads to illusory results that do not 
generalize. Genuine progress will only be possible if the ML 
community moves beyond optimizing accuracy on static 
datasets and embraces the rigorous, prospective testing stan¬≠
dards championed by the seismological community through 
frameworks like CSEP.

The necessity of standardized testing frameworks
The root cause of the discrepancies identified in this work is 
the disconnection between standard ML validation practices 
(e.g., k-fold cross-validation) and the specific requirements 
of earthquake science. To bridge this gap, the community 
must pivot from ad-hoc validation schemes to established, 
rigorous testing environments. We argue that future ML-
based forecasting research must align with well-known 
established protocols, like those defined by the Collabo¬≠
ratory for the Study of Earthquake Predictability (CSEP) 
(Zechar et¬Ýal. 2010).
By design, CSEP protocols enforce prospective (or 
pseudo-prospective) testing, where models must issue fore¬≠
casts for future time windows without access to subsequent 
data. This effectively eliminates the possibility of data leak¬≠
age. Specifically, authors recommend that ML practitioners 
adopt two core CSEP principles: 
1.	 Zero-Retraining on Test Data: In our replication, the 
random split allowed the model to "peek" at future 
events. CSEP standards require that model parameters 
be fixed before the test period begins, ensuring that the 
evaluation reflects true operational capability.
