Abstract  Accurately predicting the timing, location, 
and magnitude of earthquakes is a major global scien-
tific challenge. While machine learning methods have 
been widely used in seismic time series analysis and 
outperform traditional statistical models, their pre-
dictive capability for strong earthquakes (Mw ≥ 6.5) 
remains limited. This study develops a new predic-
tion method by constructing multi-timescale seismic 
activity features and integrating them with machine 
learning models.The novelty of this approach is a 
parameterized framework based on a historical seis-
mic window (T_history), forecast window (T_fore-
cast), and magnitude threshold (M_threshold). It 
transforms earthquake prediction into an optimization 
problem, using machine learning to model the rela-
tionship between these parameters and performance 
metrics (e.g., AUC(Area Under the (ROC) Curve)), 
and searches the parameter space to maximize AUC. 
To improve spatial prediction, clustering algorithms 
divide the study area into subregions with consist-
ent seismic characteristics, and an optimization 
algorithm determines the optimal time-magnitude 
parameters for each subregion.Using earthquake 
catalogs from Japan and New Zealand (1973–2024), 
K-means +  + was selected to partition Japan into 4 
and New Zealand into 3 subregions. With T_fore-
cast set to 365 days and time-series cross-validation 
applied to reduce overfitting, the method achieved 
average AUC values of 0.75 and 0.81 for predict-
ing strong earthquakes within one year in Japan and 
New Zealand, respectively, outperforming existing 
models.The main contribution is a flexible and exten-
sible prediction framework that overcomes the limi-
tations of fixed regions, time windows, and magni-
tude thresholds. Future work will focus on extensible

Earthquake Prediction
1  Introduction
Earthquake forecasting refers to the process of esti-
mating future seismic parameters—such as time, 
location, epicentral intensity, and magnitude—using 
scientific methods and technological means. Its 
objective is to provide a scientific basis for mitigating 
earthquake-related disasters and enhancing prepared-
ness, thereby assisting relevant authorities in taking 
proactive measures.
Earthquake prediction has long remained a major 
challenge in geophysics and seismology. Numer-
ous researchers have proposed various statistical and 
physical models in an attempt to reveal the regu-
larities of earthquake occurrence. For example, the 
Epidemic-Type Aftershock Sequence (ETAS) model 
(Ogata 1988; Helmstetter and Sornette 2003), which 
describes the patterns of mainshocks and aftershocks, 
has shown certain effectiveness in short-term fore-
casting. ETAS is based on spatiotemporal clustering 
assumptions and uses a non-homogeneous Poisson 
process to model earthquake occurrence probabilities 
(Utsu and Ogata 1995). On the other hand, physics-
based models, such as those relying on Coulomb 
stress transfer, attempt to explain earthquake trig-
gering mechanisms from the perspective of fault 
interactions and stress evolution (King et  al. 1994; 
Stein 1999). Additionally, statistical learning models, 
including the “Every Earthquake a Precursor Accord-
ing to Scale” (EEPAS) model (Rhoades and Evison 
2006) and the Pattern Informatics (PI) method (Nanjo 
et al. 2006), have advanced mid- to long-term proba-
bilistic earthquake forecasting from different angles.
However, these models still exhibit clear limita-
tions. ETAS-type models are constrained by statistical 
laws and cannot predict seismic activities that devi-
ate from these patterns; they also perform poorly in 
tectonically complex regions or in long-term forecast-
ing. It is worth noting that while the standard ETAS 
model is primarily designed for short-term (hours to 
days) forecasting, its underlying framework has been 
extended to inform longer-term probabilistic seismic 
hazard assessment (PSHA) and disaster-mitigation 
strategies by incorporating temporal clustering effects 
(e.g., Yaghmaei-Sabegh et al. 2017, 2022).
While physics-based models offer clear mecha-
nisms, they rely on many simplifying assumptions 
and require high computational resources, resulting 
in limited generalizability in practical prediction. 
Traditional statistical methods are inefficient in han-
dling high-dimensional and multi-source data and 
struggle to capture nonlinear dynamic behaviors in 
seismic systems. Thus, despite the variety of models 
proposed, earthquake prediction remains a largely 
unsolved scientific problem.
In recent years, the rapid development of machine 
learning has led to its increasing application in seis-
mic research (Mousavi et al. 2020; Borate et al. 2023; 
Lin et  al. 2023; Shan et  al. 2022; Seydoux et  al. 
2020). Machine learning offers significant advantages 
in data processing and nonlinear modeling, particu-
larly with high-dimensional and complex data. Some 
studies have demonstrated that machine learning out-
performs traditional statistical methods; for instance, 
the RECAST model, trained on large historical 
earthquake catalogs, has surpassed ETAS in predic-
tive performance (Dascher-Cousineau et  al. 2023). 
Notably, significant progress has also been made in 
the engineering applications of machine learning for 
disaster management, where it has been successfully 
employed for the real-time or near-real-time estima-
tion of crucial parameters such as earthquake magni-
tude, location, peak ground acceleration, peak ground 
velocity, and response spectra (Liu et al. 2024; Shokr-
gozar-Yatimdar and Chen 2025). These advances 
primarily rely on multi-parameter features extracted 
from large-scale seismic datasets and advanced deep 
learning architectures, thereby offering new data-
driven solutions for earthquake early warning and 
engineering seismic design (Wang et  al. 2023; Xi 
et al. 2024).
Current research can be broadly categorized into 
classification and regression tasks. In classification-
based prediction, studies have combined support 
vector regression (SVR) and hybrid neural networks 
(HNN) with enhanced particle swarm optimization 
(EPSO) to predict earthquakes above magnitude 
5.0 in specific regions (Asim et  al. 2018). Other 
approaches include using artificial neural networks 
(ANN) for site classification (Yaghmaei-Sabegh &

Tsang 2011), and applying support vector machines 
(SVM), random forests (RF), and ANN on clustered 
data to predict earthquakes above different magni-
tude thresholds (Asim et al. 2020). A common trait 
among these studies is that they only output a binary 
judgment of earthquake occurrence without providing 
specific numerical information such as magnitude, 
time, or location. For regression tasks, which aim to 
predict specific numerical parameters like magnitude, 
time, depth, or location, techniques such as random 
forest (RF), multilayer perceptron (MLP), and support 
vector regression (SVR) have been used to predict 
magnitude (Jain et al. 2021). Some studies employed 
gated recurrent units (GRU) to predict depth, time, 
and magnitude (Gonzalez et  al. 2021), while others 
applied long short-term memory (LSTM) networks 
to forecast future earthquake magnitudes (Berhich 
et al. 2021). These regression-based studies generally 
exhibit poor performance and low accuracy in pre-
dicting specific values.
Furthermore, some research utilizes machine 
learning to analyze multi-source seismic data—such 
as GPS, groundwater, and geomagnetic data—in an 
attempt to predict earthquakes by identifying anoma-
lous signals (Wang et al. 2020; Ruan et al. 2023; Pei 
Huang et al. 2024; Schimmenti et al. 2024; Tsuchiya 
et  al. 2024). However, such data are often difficult 
to obtain and have limited spatiotemporal cover-
age, which restricts the broader application of these 
methods.
In contrast, seismic activity indicators—such as 
the b-value and a-value derived from earthquake cata-
logs (Gutenberg and Richter 1944)—are relatively 
accessible and have been commonly used in research 
(Köhler et al. 2023; Herrmann et al. 2022; Girona and 
Drymoni 2024; Shi et  al. 2018). However, existing 
studies often fail to effectively utilize the spatiotem-
poral information of these indicators across multiple 
time scales and regional contexts.
To address the above challenges, this study pro-
poses a novel classification framework for earthquake 
prediction based on multi-parameter optimization 
and regional clustering. In addition to improving 
classification performance, the framework provides 
information on future occurrence time, location, 
and magnitude, relying solely on earthquake catalog 
data to systematically enhance classification predic-
tion performance based on seismic activity data. The 
innovativeness of this method is mainly reflected in 
the following aspects:
First, unlike previous studies that often relied on 
physical intuition or empirical settings for key param-
eters, this study transforms the selection of the histor-
ical time window (T_history), forecast time window 
(T_forecast), and magnitude threshold (M_thresh-
old) into an optimizable three-dimensional param-
eter search problem. A method combining random 
search and greedy algorithms is employed to empiri-
cally identify the best parameter combination in dif-
ferent regions, thereby enhancing the model’s adapt-
ability to regional specificities. Second, to capture 
spatial heterogeneity in seismic activity, clustering 
algorithms—including K-means +  +, DBSCAN, and 
GMM—are introduced to objectively partition the 
study area and identify tectonic subregions with con-
sistent seismic characteristics. For each subregion, a 
separate random forest classification model is estab-
lished, improving regional prediction specificity and 
mitigating the inadequacy of a single global model in 
responding to local dynamics. Moreover, addressing 
the extreme class imbalance in earthquake catalogs, 
this study uses AUC as the core metric for model 
optimization and evaluation, making it more suitable 
for high-cost-of-miss scenarios in earthquake predic-
tion. Time-series cross-validation is applied to ensure 
model generalizability and avoid performance overes-
timation due to overfitting.
By analyzing seismic data from Japan and New 
Zealand between 1973 and 2024, the model success-
fully partitioned the study areas into 4 and 3 clustered 
subregions, respectively, and achieved high classifi-
cation prediction performance. In comparisons with 
other models, the proposed model outperformed 
existing approaches. This method offers new insights 
and perspectives for earthquake prediction.
2  Data
In this study, we utilized earthquake records from 
Japan and New Zealand collected by the United 
States Geological Survey (USGS). Both countries are 
located within the seismically active Pacific Ring of 
Fire, providing a sufficient number of seismic events 
to facilitate analysis of the mechanisms and spati-
otemporal distribution characteristics of earthquakes 
across different magnitudes.

The spatial distribution of the selected earthquakes 
is shown in Fig.  1. To ensure the completeness of 
the earthquake catalog, we applied a sliding window 
approach to determine the completeness magnitude 
(Mc). Each window contains 300 seismic events. We 
used the Frequency-Magnitude Distribution (FMD)

It should be noted that earthquake records prior to 
1973 lack a sufficient number of small earthquakes. 
Therefore, data from 1900 to 1973 were primarily 
used to augment the dataset for defining clustered 
regions and were excluded from the training data-
set. The training and testing data were selected from 
the period between 1973 and 2024. Specifically, the 
moment magnitude (Mw) was used throughout this 
study.
We adopted 16 activity indicators as features for 
training data. These features were directly input into 
the machine learning model for training. Clustering 
partitions were based on longitude and latitude infor-
mation from the earthquake catalog, which required 
normalization. Training data were generated accord-
ing to the partitions, meaning that 16 activity indi-
cators were generated for each region. The random 
forest model is insensitive to the scale and range of 
feature values and thus did not require normaliza-
tion. The features include b-value and a-value, seis-
mic energy release, root energy of seismic activity, 
latitude and longitude variance, epicenter density, 
epicenter centroid, seismic frequency, maximum and 
mean magnitude, energy-weighted epicenter centroid, 
maximum magnitude deficit, and latitude–longitude 
slope. Detailed calculation procedures are provided in 
Supplementary Material S1.
3  Method
3.1  Regional partitioning
First, we applied multiple clustering algorithms to 
partition earthquake records from Japan and New 
Zealand between 1900 and 2024 into subregions, 
including K-means +  + (Arthur & Vassilvitskii 2006), 
DBSCAN (Schubert et al. 2017), and Gaussian Mix-
ture Models (GMM) (Bouman et  al. 1997). The 
results are shown in Fig. 2.
It is worth noting that the K-means +  + algorithm 
relies on a single hyperparameter—the number of 
clusters (k)—which simplifies the optimization pro-
cess and avoids the additional complexity associated 
with multiple hyperparameters required by other 
clustering methods. Moreover, K-means +  + is an

3.2  Training and optimization
We constructed a dedicated objective function for 
the earthquake prediction task. This objective func-
tion incorporates three input parameters: the past time 
window (T_history), the forecast time window (T_
forecast), and the magnitude threshold (M_threshold).
The past time window (T_history) refers to the size 
of the time window used to compute the 16 features, 
which is the parameter required for feature genera-
tion. This window always precedes the forecast time 
window (T_forecast). The forecast time window 
(T_forecast) represents the time window used to com-
pute binary classification labels, i.e., the length of the 
future period to be predicted. The magnitude thresh-
old (M_threshold) parameter defines the threshold 
magnitude for binary classification of future seismic 
events; events exceeding this magnitude are labeled 
as 1 (occurrence).
These parameters enable the generation of multi-
scale time-series data, capturing seismic activ-
ity across different temporal scales and magnitude 
ranges, thereby providing a basis for predicting 
whether future events will exceed the specified mag-
nitude threshold. The objective function evaluates the 
classification performance of the model under dif-
ferent parameter combinations, using metrics such 
as AUC and F1-score as output values to assess and

ally, extensive data preprocessing, such as addressing

4  Results
4.1  Clustering partitions for Japan and New Zealand
We employed grid search to select optimal parame-
ters by maximizing the Silhouette Score. The param-
eter combinations, Silhouette Scores, and specific 
clustering results for the two algorithms are presented 
in Fig. 2 and Table 1.
For the K-means +  + algorithm, the highest Silhou-
ette Score was achieved with 1 cluster (i.e., no parti-
tioning), while the score decreased gradually from 1 
to 4 clusters and dropped significantly beyond 4 clus-
ters. Considering that smaller partitions may lead to 
better prediction performance, we set the number of 
clusters for Japan to 4 and for New Zealand to 3 in the 
K-means +  + algorithm.
Analysis of the results (as shown in Table 1 and 
Fig.  2) indicates that both DBSCAN and GMM 
algorithms were ineffective in partitioning the entire 
earthquake catalogs of Japan and New Zealand, 
whereas K-means +  + proved to be simple and effec-
tive. Table 1 compares the best results obtained from 
various clustering algorithms. Based on comprehen-
sive evaluation, K-means +  + was identified as the 
most effective method. The four partitions for Japan 
align well with its actual geological context: Region 
0 corresponds to the junction area of the Pacific Plate, 
Philippine Sea Plate, and Eurasian Plate; Region 1 
represents the seismic belt formed by the Philippine 
Sea Plate and Eurasian Plate; Region 2 corresponds 
to the seismic belt formed by the Philippine Sea Plate 
and Pacific Plate; and Region 3 represents the seismic 
belt formed by the Eurasian Plate and Pacific Plate.
For Japan, the DBSCAN algorithm excluded some 
so-called noise points, while GMM broadly divided 
the data into two large regions, which were too coarse 
for prediction tasks. For New Zealand, the DBSCAN 
algorithm also ignored some noise points, whereas 
the GMM algorithm produced the same three-region 
partition as the K-means +  + algorithm.
The selection of k = 4 for Japan and k = 3 for New 
Zealand in the K-means +  + algorithm represents a 
balance between two objectives: maintaining a rela-
tively high Silhouette Score (indicating well-defined 
clusters) and creating smaller, spatially finer parti-
tions for the binary classification prediction task. As 
shown in Supplementary Figs. S3 and S4, the Silhou-
ette Scores for both regions exhibit a gradual decline, 
yet at the selected k values remain at an acceptable 
level while also providing a smaller partition scale. 
This ensures meaningful cluster separation while 
avoiding the sharper decline in the score observed 
when the number of partitions increases further.

4.2  Prediction results
We first divided the seismic events into training set 
D_train and testing set D_test at an 8:2 ratio (Yadav 
and Shukla 2016). The training set was exclusively 
used to train the random forest model f_θ, ensur-
ing that the model was never exposed to the test set. 
To prevent overfitting, all evaluation metrics were 
computed on the test set using time-series cross-val-
idation. Additionally, the number of random forest 
parameters was designed not to exceed the size of the 
smallest training subset. The final settings included 
up to 300 trees with a maximum depth of 8 per tree to 
mitigate both overfitting and underfitting.
After obtaining the optimal parameter combina-
tion, this study also conducted a test comparison on 
the optimization of the random forest hyperparameter 
n_estimators, as shown in supplementary material 
tables S1 and S2.
The forecast time window (T_forecast) was fixed 
at 365 days (one year) to ensure meaningful predic-
tion horizons. The magnitude threshold (M_thresh-
old) was varied within the range [5.0, 7.5], and the 
past time window length (T_history) was explored 
within [100, 2048] days.
This study first focused on the optimal perfor-
mance of the model within each subregion. The 
results for the best-performing parameter combina-
tions on the test set are detailed in Figs. 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 
9 and 10 and Table 2. Model prediction performance 
was rigorously evaluated using AUC, ROC curves, 
and precision-recall curves to ensure a comprehen-
sive assessment.
As shown in Table  2, AUC values were com-
puted for Japan and New Zealand using three dif-
ferent clustering methods. The final AUC value for 
each region was obtained by averaging the results 
from threefold cross-validation to prevent overfit-
ting. The rationale for this approach is as follows: 
Given the nature of our forecasting task—predicting 
rare, high-magnitude events—setting aside a single, 
contiguous time period as a traditional "independ-
ent test set" poses a significant challenge. Due to

4.3  Comparison results
Currently, AI-based earthquake prediction methods 
lack representative seismic datasets and appropriate 
baseline methods, hindering researchers’ ability to 
compare their approaches and results (Banna et  al. 
2020). To evaluate our work, we adapted a study con-
ducting seismic classification prediction in the same 
region of Japan (Berhich et  al. 2023) by modifying 
the magnitude threshold to 5 for parameter optimi-
zation and comparing the resulting AUC values. As 
shown in Table 3, that study also divided Japan into 
three regions and used accuracy to evaluate the spa-
tial distribution of earthquakes above magnitude 5. 
Due to differences in region numbering, the results 
were reindexed to align with the numbering used in 
this study. The comparison revealed that our model 
achieved higher accuracy in predicting earthquakes 
above magnitude 5 in Region 0 and Region 3, while 
performance in Region 1 was comparable. Addition-
ally, we reported the accuracy corresponding to the 
best AUC values obtained in our study, which demon-
strated partial performance in predicting some major 
earthquakes.
We also compared our results with a study predict-
ing seismic events across the entire region of Japan 
(Zhang and Wang 2023). As shown in Table 4, that 
study evaluated precision and recall for predicting 
earthquakes above magnitude 4 occurring between 
March 2020 and May 2021. However, our data con-
struction approach was not suitable for direct com-
parison using this methodology, as earthquakes above 
magnitude 4 occur frequently in Japan. Instead, we 
compared the average precision and recall values for 
predicting larger earthquakes in each subregion over 
the test set (approximately 10 years). The results indi-
cate that our method achieved higher recall for larger 
magnitude thresholds and longer prediction periods, 
though with slightly lower precision.

5  Discussions
In this study, we employed a combined approach of 
clustering algorithms, random forests, and optimiza-
tion methods to uncover underlying mechanisms in 
seismic activity data.
Earthquake 
prediction 
research 
encompasses 
diverse time scales, ranging from short-term predic-
tions (hours to days) to decadal-scale forecasts aimed 
at long-term hazard risk assessment (Kanamori 
2003). We defined the prediction window as one 
year (365 days). This fixed time frame is particularly 
useful for accurately assessing risks in catastrophe 
insurance, optimizing premium pricing, and prepar-
ing claim reserves. For national disaster risk reduc-
tion and mitigation planning, this method can inform 
resource allocation and the determination of seis-
mic coefficients, ultimately improving post-disaster 
response efficiency.
It is instructive to explicitly compare our machine-
learning-based framework with existing short-term 
forecasting approaches, particularly the widely used 
Epidemic-Type Aftershock Sequence (ETAS) mod-
els. Both approaches aim to leverage statistical pat-
terns in historical seismicity to forecast future activ-
ity. The core similarity lies in their reliance on the 
self-exciting nature of seismicity, where past events 
influence future probabilities. However, fundamental 
differences exist in model assumptions and temporal 
applicability. Standard ETAS models are paramet-
ric, explicitly built on specific triggering laws (e.g., 
Omori-Utsu) and designed primarily for short-term 
forecasting (hours to weeks) within sequences, focus-
ing on the evolution of aftershock rates. In contrast, 
our data-driven, non-parametric framework does 
not assume a specific functional form for triggering. 
It is designed for longer-term (year-scale) forecast-
ing by learning complex, high-dimensional patterns 
from a broader set of seismic activity indicators. This 
distinction positions our method as complementary 
to ETAS models: while ETAS excels at modeling 
detailed short-term sequence evolution, our approach 
targets the identification of regions with enhanced 
probability of significant events over a longer, fixed 
future window, aligning more closely with appli-
cations in probabilistic seismic hazard assessment 
(PSHA) that incorporate time-dependent clustering 
effects over extended periods (e.g., Šipčić et al. 2022; 
Shokrgozar-Yatimdar et al. 2025).
This study is based on a hypothesis in earthquake 
prediction that past seismic activity patterns can, to 
some extent, reflect future earthquake occurrences 
(Utsu 2002; Kanamori 1981). However, a long-stand-
ing challenge has been how to quantify this "memory 
effect"—specifically, determining the length of the 
historical time window (T_history) that can effec-
tively predict a given future window (T_forecast), 
and how to identify the optimal magnitude thresh-
old (M_threshold). Previous studies often fixed these 
parameters based on physical intuition or empirical 
experience (Mousavi and Beroza 2023; Asim et  al. 
2020; Qin et al. 2022). In contrast, a core contribu-
tion of this study is the systematic use of a method 
combining random search and greedy algorithms to 
empirically explore the combinatorial relationships of 
these three key parameters, thereby identifying opti-
mal prediction configurations for different regions.
In terms of model selection, although deep learn-
ing models (e.g., DNN, CNN, RNN) show strong 
potential in sequence prediction tasks (Mousavi and 
Beroza 2023), their performance heavily depends on 
large amounts of data and meticulous hyperparameter 
tuning. Given the limited size of earthquake catalogs 
and our emphasis on model robustness, we selected 
the random forest model, which has fewer hyperpa-
rameters and lower requirements for data preprocess-
ing. This model has proven highly effective for clas-
sification tasks in the geosciences (Kong et al. 2019).
Regarding performance evaluation, we faced 
inherent trade-offs among metrics. Due to dataset 
imbalance, we chose AUC as the core optimization 
objective. AUC provides an overall measure of model 
performance across all possible classification thresh-
olds, which is crucial in scenarios where positive 
samples (large earthquakes) are extremely rare and 
misclassification costs are high. In contrast, thresh-
old-dependent metrics such as F1-score are more 
contingent on specific choices, while Accuracy can be 
severely misleading with imbalanced data.

Although our dataset focuses on prediction within 
a one-year timeframe and does not provide detailed 
information on the effects of different time ranges 
across subregions, we believe our optimization frame-
work and analytical approach can be effectively 
applied to a wide variety of scenarios involving fixed 
magnitude thresholds, time windows, and spatial 
regions. Throughout this process, overfitting must 
be effectively prevented, and extrapolation via time-
series cross-validation is an effective method.
We employed time-series cross-validation to strictly 
prevent overfitting, which is a recommended approach 
for assessing the generalization ability of temporal 
models (Vamsikrishna et al. 2024). The results clearly 
show significant differences in the optimal parameters 
for different subregions. We speculate that these differ-
ences stem from varying earthquake preparation mech-
anisms across tectonic units (Lu and Cao 2021).
Currently, this study relies solely on earthquake 
catalog data. Significant future improvements could be 
achieved by integrating multi-source geophysical data. 
For example, many studies have shown that surface 
observation data, such as crustal deformation (GPS/
GNSS) and changes in the geomagnetic field, have the 
potential to reveal pre-seismic physical processes (Shah 
2022). Our machine learning framework is highly exten-
sible and can accommodate these new feature dimen-
sions, which will be a focus of future work. Simultane-
ously, exploring more advanced optimization algorithms 
and regularization techniques to approach global optimal 
solutions will also be a key direction for future research.
This method uses a data-driven approach to objec-
tively determine the optimal prediction parameters 
(T_history, M_threshold) for different tectonic regions, 
enhancing the regional adaptability of the forecasting 
model. The output results can be used to identify areas 
with enhanced seismic activity within the next year, pro-
viding auxiliary reference for seismic trend consultations 
and the delineation of key monitoring areas. Since only 
earthquake catalogs are used, this method can be imple-
mented in any region with earthquake catalog records.
6  Conclusion
In this study, we proposed a novel approach for binary 
earthquake prediction by constructing an objective 
optimization function incorporating three parameters: 
the past time window (T_history), the magnitude 
threshold (M_threshold), and the forecast time 
window (T_forecast). Clustering algorithms were 
employed to determine subregions, and a random for-
est model was used to optimize the objective function 
value. The following conclusions were drawn:
(1)	 The entire methodological framework is based on 
a core hypothesis that seismic activity in a past 
period can reflect the intensity of future earth-
quake occurrences. Different tectonic regions and 
seismic activities lead to entirely distinct optimal 
parameters and optimization outcomes.
(2)	 Among the three clustering methods tested—
K-means +  +, 
DBSCAN, 
and 
GMM—the 
K-means +  + algorithm yielded the best perfor-
mance. The partitioning results for Japan were gen-
erally consistent with the distribution of major fault 
zones.
(3)	 Comparative analysis with existing studies demon-
strated that the proposed method performs effec-
tively in predicting highly imbalanced datasets 
involving earthquakes above magnitude 6.5 or even 
larger. The method proved applicable across differ-
ent regions, as validated by results from both Japan 
and New Zealand. For subregions with suboptimal 
performance, future work should involve more 
advanced optimization algorithms and broader, 
more reasonable parameter ranges. While this 
study relied on 16 features derived from earthquake 
catalogs—such as seismic energy and spatial char-
acteristics—future improvements could incorpo-
rate multi-source data for enhanced prediction.
