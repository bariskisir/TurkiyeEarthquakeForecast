Abstract
Engineers must assess soil susceptibility to liquefaction, yet conventional evalu­
ations are time‑consuming, costly, and affected by field-testing variability and 
semi‑empirical tools that introduce uncertainty. This study clarifies the importance 
of key parameters and proposes a streamlined machine‑learning approach using 
ensemble methods. A comprehensive reference dataset was compiled and used to 
train reliable machine‑learning models. Feature importance was examined with lo­
gistic regression and random forest, after which the data were split into training 
and test sets, predictors were scaled, and hyperparame0ters were tuned with Grid­
SearchCV. Advanced models were then fitted, followed by ensemble approaches, 
including AdaBoost and voting classifiers. Based on feature importance results, the 
most influential features across all methods continue to be the Standard Penetration 
Test-derived parameters. The trained models were assessed, in which the AdaBoost 
provided the most accurate estimations by achieving precision, recall, F1_score, 
Jaccard index, and accuracy of 88%, 88%, 88%, 79%, and 88%, respectively. The 
Voting Classifier demonstrated superior performance over the AdaBoost Classifier 
in terms of lower false negative values and higher true positive values, which could 
be considered as a better predictor for high-risk cases.
Keywords  Liquefaction · Ensemble methods · Machine learning · Vulnerability 
assessment · Feature importance

It is evident that FSLiq is influenced by various parameters associated with soil resis­

introduces sensitivity to the assumptions and simplifications inherent in their estima­
tion. Cubrinovski and Ntritsos (2023) observed that following the 2010–2011 Can­
terbury (New Zealand) earthquakes, while simplified methods, primarily based on a 
pseudo-static approach using FS against liquefaction triggering, generally provided 
reasonable predictions of liquefaction manifestation and the overall extent of damage, 
there were numerous cases where these methods produced results inconsistent with 
field observations. Cubrinovski et al. (2019) identified the primary reason for these 
mispredictions as the failure of simplified analyses to account for dynamic interac­
tions and system response effects, which are critical in accurately assessing liquefac­
tion potential. While conducting additional tests is suggested by some researchers, 
acquiring undisturbed field samples for this purpose can be complex and expensive 
(Seed et al. 1982). Additionally, replicating in-situ stress conditions and scale effects 
combined are among other challenges for laboratory-based approaches. These limita­
tions may provide an opportunity for using advanced methods in support of the more 
traditional geotechnical procedures, such as machine learning-based approaches, to 
integrate diverse datasets, model complex interactions, and improve prediction accu­
racy by learning from field and laboratory data to achieve higher accuracy in lique­
faction assessments.
The use of artificial neural network (ANN) and multiple linear regression (MLR) 
has shown promising outcomes in predicting both detachability and the liquefaction 
potential index (DLPI), as proposed by Egbueri et al. (2023). Furthermore, recent 
research by Aytaş et al. (2023) has highlighted the correlation between soil char­
acteristics, earthquake parameters, and liquefaction risk, suggesting it as a practi­
cal indicator for assessing liquefaction potential. In recent years, Machine Learning 
(ML) has gained considerable attention as a multidisciplinary subfield of Artificial 
Intelligence to analyze complex datasets and discover previously unknown relation­
ships for the purpose of soil liquefaction prediction (Idriss and Boulanger 2008). 
Machine Learning (ML) techniques can be useful in predicting soil liquefaction as 
they offer a flexible and accurate approach to this problem. Extensive datasets can be 
analyzed using ML algorithms to identify patterns and relationships that traditional 
methods may miss, leading to improved accuracy in predicting soil liquefaction and 
associated potential damage to surrounding structures. Various ML approaches such

improve the quality of reasoning and solutions’ adaptivity. Hence, ensemble and 
hybrid models are employed in several real-world problems, including civil engi­

vided promising results in the prediction of liquefaction potential index (LPI) as a 
continuous variable. Khatti et al. (2024) introduced an optimal performance model 
for predicting the liquefaction potential using 170 results of cone penetration test­
ing, using two baselines, thirty tree-based, thirty SVC-based, and fifteen NN-based 
models.

2.3  Machine Learning (ML) Algorithms
In recent years, the use of machine learning (ML) algorithms has gained popularity 
in various scientific fields. These algorithms establish accurate relationships between 
input and output variables based on prior experience gained through a training 
process.
This study employed a labeled dataset to train five different ML algorithms for 
predicting liquefaction occurrence. The algorithms used include (1) support vector 
machine, (2) stochastic gradient descent, (3) multi-layer perceptron neural network, 
(4) K neighbors, and (5) decision tree, which were implemented in Python using the 
scikit-learn toolbox. These algorithms have demonstrated effectiveness in providing 
solutions to civil engineering issues (e.g., Alishvandi et al. 2024) which make them 
an ideal choice for this study to be fed into ensemble approaches. Because the fun­
damentals of the employed machine‑learning algorithms have been widely covered 
in the literature, the following sections briefly describe only the ensemble methods, 
cross‑validation, and hyperparameter‑tuning procedures, and the evaluation metrics.

rate and robust prediction by combining different models or techniques (Dixit 2017). 
However, it should be noted that ensemble methods can be computationally expen­
sive and require more resources. In this research, two ensemble models were tested, 
and their results will be discussed in the following sections. In the current investiga­

2.4.1  Adaptive Boosting Classifier (AdaBoost)
The adaptive boosting algorithm, commonly known as AdaBoost, was proposed by 
Freund and Schapire in 1997 as an iterative method for constructing a “strong” clas­
sifier. AdaBoost adaptively adjusts the errors of weak classifiers obtained from mul­
tiple learners. The main concept behind the AdaBoost is to employ various weak 
classifiers and subsequently combine them into a stronger classifier. To achieve this, 
the data distribution is modified, and the weight of each sample is adjusted based on 
its correct classification and overall classification accuracy. The updated dataset with 
modified weights is then sent to the next classifier in the sequence for further training. 
Finally, the classifiers generated by each training iteration are combined to create the 
final decision classifier. The AdaBoost classifier has several advantages, including its 
ability to eliminate unnecessary training data features and prioritize critical training 
data. Furthermore, AdaBoost is a simple and effective technique for enhancing weak 
classification algorithms, which can improve data classification abilities by reducing 
both bias and variance through continuous training (Wu et al. 2020). In the present 
study, the Decision Tree Classifier was chosen as the base estimator for AdaBoost.
2.4.2  Voting Classifier (VC)
Employing a voting classifier consists of adopting a number of base ML classifi­
ers, each of which is fit to the considered dataset. Consequently, the final prediction 
is obtained by averaging predictions made by the base learners (Pedregosa et al. 
2011). Considering this approach can be beneficial in cases where algorithms are 
performing well, as it can help to reduce individual weaknesses and provide a more 
robust estimation (Kilimci 2022). To achieve a more precise prediction of liquefac­
tion occurrence, the VC was employed with its default values using all five of the 
aforementioned ML techniques.
2.5  Cross-validation and Hyperparameter Tuning
In order to assess the efficacy of machine learning algorithms, it is customary to 
set aside a portion of the input data as a test dataset, denoted as X_test and Y_test. 
In the present study, the scikit-learn recommended approach of train_test_split was 
employed to partition the original dataset into train (80%) and test (20%) subsets. 
However, manually selecting hyperparameters can sometimes result in suboptimal 
performance of the designated estimators and lead to overfitting on the test dataset. 
To address this issue, Cross-Validation (CV) is often used, wherein the training set is 
divided into k parts. The model is trained using k-1 of the folds, while the remaining 
part is utilized for evaluation purposes (Pedregosa et al. 2011). The workflow adopted 
in this study is depicted in Fig. 6.

3  Results and Discussions
In this study, ensemble methods, namely AdaBoost and voting classifiers, were uti­
lized to improve the performance of previously trained ML methods. The evaluation

4  Limitations and Suggestions
The current investigation has been conducted based on the provided dataset by Cetin 
et al. (2018). Consequently, the learned functions and feature importance results 
reflect the statistical properties of the provided records.
The employed machine‑learning approaches were tuned using a range of accept­
able values for the hyperparameter search space, as listed in Table 2. The models 
were therefore optimized within these bounds. Expanding the search space would 
significantly increase training time and cost.
Expanding access to a broader and more diverse dataset is essential for advanc­
ing predictive models for liquefaction. By incorporating data from a wider range of 
cases, geographic regions, and soil types, future research can facilitate the develop­
ment of models that are more universally applicable. Utilizing globally distributed 
datasets will enable the creation of more robust and accurate machine learning mod­
els tailored for liquefaction prediction.
Moreover, while this study has utilized various machine learning algorithms, there 
is significant potential in exploring alternative approaches, such as metaheuristic 
methods. These techniques could offer innovative solutions and uncover new insights 
into data patterns, enhancing the predictive capabilities of liquefaction models from 
different perspectives.
Future research should also prioritize hyperparameter optimization for each 
machine learning model. A detailed exploration of the hyperparameter space could 
illuminate the sensitivity of models to specific configurations, ultimately improving 
prediction accuracy and ensuring greater model reliability.

This study aimed to evaluate the ability of seven algorithms (including ML and 
ensemble) to predict liquefaction accurately. Additionally, the study investigated the 
impact of each considered parameter on the occurrence of liquefaction using fea­

2.	 CSR parameters (e.g., CSR(σv′, Mw) and its variations) are ranked among the most

analysis.
3.	 While parameters like amax, Mw, and FC are moderately important, in contrast,

6.	 AdaBoost outperformed the other ML methods, with a precision, recall, F1_score, 
Jaccard index, and accuracy of 88%, 88%, 88%, 79%, and 88%, respectively.
7.	 SGD and NN_MLPC had roughly a similar prediction performance, with an 
accuracy of 83%.
8.	 DT classifier had the poorest performance in predicting liquefaction occurrence, 
with an accuracy of 69%, while KNN and Voting classifiers provided estimations

9.	 Ensemble methods were effective in improving the predictions using weak learn­
