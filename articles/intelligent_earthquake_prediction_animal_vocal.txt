analysis based on machine learning

Earthquakes are natural calamities that are destructive and result in the loss of lives and economic 
losses globally. It is vital to identify earthquakes at an early stage to reduce the damage caused to 
the environment and infrastructure. But, traditional monitoring much lacks timely warnings. A study 
has suggested an innovative framework that utilizes Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning (DL) 
to identify animal behaviour to predict earthquakes. This research proposes a novel framework to 
identify earthquake precursors using the field of bioacoustics, and ML and DL models. Animal’s audio 
recordings of historical animal vocalizations were enhanced using data augmentation techniques to 
increase the audio dataset. The library utilized Librosa for extracting temporal and spectral features 
from the audio data. The ML algorithms used in the model were XGBoost, Random Forest, and 
Multi-Layer Perceptron. The DL algorithms used were Recurrent Neural Networks, Long Short-Term 
Memory, Bidirectional LSTM, and Gated Recurrent Units. The model was trained using a ratio of 
80:20. Hyperparameter tuning and early stopping were used to improve the model’s performance. DL 
methods have shown their potential to go beyond the conventional ML methods in handling temporal 
dependencies of the sequential audio data. The Bi-LSTM model obtained a test accuracy of 98.87%, 
and the AUC was found to be close to 1.00, which shows the effectiveness of the model in terms of 
generalization and robustness to environmental noises. The scalability of the model was tested using 
unseen datasets. The proposed approach permits a cost-effective early warning system. The approach 
is more beneficial in areas where there is no advance infrastructure available for seismic activity 
detection. Future work will be focused on the integration of IoT technology with edge computing 
technology to improve the scalability of the system.
Keywords  Animal behavioural patterns, Earthquake precursor detection, Data augmentation, Machine 
learning, Deep learning
Natural calamities like earthquakes, floods, and storms pose a major threat to the lives and properties of 
people around the globe. These calamities have the potential to cause damage to properties, leaving people in 
a vulnerable position. In some cases, these calamities may prove to be fatal. The unpredictable nature of these 
calamities adds to the problems of mitigating and managing them. Earthquakes prove to be calamities of great 
destruction due to their sudden nature, resulting in huge damage and loss of life every year1. The 2010 Haiti 
earthquake resulted in over 230,000 fatalities2. In China, the 2008 earthquake caused the loss of 87,000 lives3. 
In Nepal, the 2015 earthquake caused the loss of 9000 lives, with many injured and the destruction of more

show the level of damage that can be caused by an earthquake. The Gujarat earthquake that took place in 2001 
in India resulted in over 20,000 deaths5. This tragedy caused injuries and displaced a large number of people. 
This accident also pointed out the importance of effective earthquake mitigation techniques. This includes the 
importance of early warnings. The aim is to ensure that there are no deaths or losses.

Literature review
The prediction of earthquake precursor prediction is considered to be one of the most challenging issues in 
the subject of seismology. Though conventional methods are based on geophysical parameters, recent research 
has also investigated new approaches like ML and DL models that use unusual animal behaviours to predict 
earthquakes. This literature review is based on the major contributions made in these fields.
Bhargava et al.11 sought to examine the importance of unusual behaviours in animals in earthquake 
prediction. They argued that unusual behaviours in animals can accurately sense geophysical triggers for 
earthquakes better than humans. They based their arguments on previous studies done in China, Japan, and the 
USA, which showed promising results. However, they noted the importance of obtaining data and incorporating 
unusual behaviours in animals in earthquake prediction algorithms. Liso et al.21 undertook a multi-parameter 
study in Western Piedmont, Italy. They sought to find the correlation between unusual behaviours in animals 
and earthquakes. In this study, unusual behaviours in animals were noted six hours prior to an earthquake. 
The unusual behaviours were noted in dogs and cats. The authors noted a three-stage pattern of vocalization

Methodology and materials
The methodology and materials section are based on a thorough analysis of animal vocalization data. The method 
begins by extracting 21 features using the Librosa library. To improve the training dataset, we use sophisticated 
augmentation techniques such as time stretching, pitch variation, and noise addition. The acoustic signals are 
then classified for seismic risk using Bi-LSTM and GRU networks.
Proposed model
Figure 1 shows the proposed model for earthquake related to ML and DL for animal’s voice behaviours. The 
system identifies earthquake precursors based on the analysis of variations in animal vocal behaviour. The system 
applies both ML and DL techniques. Recordings of animal voices are obtained under normal and abnormal 
situations, pre-processed, and converted to acoustic features. The features are labelled and stored for evaluation; 
the data is divided into a training and testing set. DL models with a sequential structure (RNN, LSTM, GRU, Bi-
LSTM) and ML models (RF, MLP, XGBoost) are trained to detect abnormal vocal behaviour. The best model is 
used to classify unknown animal voices as either normal or abnormal to show possible earthquakes. Evaluation 
metrics such as Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, and AUC are used.

Frequency analysis (Fig. 3) discloses complex acoustic patterns in animal vocalizations. The strongest energy 
peak is located near 2506 Hz (around − 41.6 dB), with additional peaks at 100 Hz, 390 Hz, and 1000 Hz. Based on 
a Hann window function, the animal vocalizations have multiple frequency components that determine timbre 
and loudness51,52. The paper also includes a table that summarizes audio feature extraction for animal vocal 
signals, with a focus on the most important acoustic features and their role in ML and DL.
The raw signal s(t) is applied to a Hann window and then transformed into its power spectrum through 
FFT. The prominent peaks are at 100 Hz, 390 Hz, and 2506 Hz. To better represent how we perceive sound, the 
spectrum is then converted to the Mel scale through triangular filters53. For each Mel band, the log of its energy 
is computed to represent human sensitivity to loudness. Then, a Discrete Cosine Transform is performed on the 
log Mel-energies, and the resulting MFCCs are obtained. In the current dataset, MFCCs mfcc_1 to mfcc_13, 
along with zero-crossing rate (zcr) and spectral centroid, are used as 21 features to train an ML and DL model 
to predict earthquake risk.
The analysis of barking patterns (Fig. 4) before an earthquake shows a stressed acoustic pattern. The statistics 
speak for themselves: average frequency of 474.9 Hz, with high-frequency spikes reaching 630 Hz, which is 
indicative of alarm barking. The prominent frequency of 2506 Hz indicates an urgent, sharp acoustic signal. The 
stability measures indicate jitter of 5.96% and shimmer of 21.63%, which is indicative of pronounced pitch and 
amplitude variations due to fear. The audio signal contains 111 consecutive pulses in 0.291 s.

Statistical Validation and Cross-Validation Analysis of Proposed Bi-LSTM Model
A k-fold cross-validation with k = 5 was used to examine the model robustness and generalization. The Bi-LSTM 
model achieved a mean accuracy of 98.72%. The standard deviation was ± 0.31, indicating a steady performance 
across different data splits. The evaluation metrics were assigned 95% confidence intervals (CIs). The Bi-LSTM 
model scored a 95% confidence interval of [98.21%, 99.12%] for accuracy, demonstrating consistent predictions 
across various sample conditions. Precision and memory also showed narrow confidence ranges. Paired t-tests 
were used to assess the statistical significance of the proposed Bi-LSTM model compared to the baseline models 
(RF, XGBoost, and LSTM). The Bi-LSTM model showed statistically significant improvements (p < 0.01). For 
comparing the effectiveness of the Bi-LSTM approach compared with the other two baselines (RF and XGBoost), 
as well as the conventional LSTM model, paired t-tests were applied. The findings revealed a statistically significant 
superiority (p < 0.01) with regard to the Bi-LSTM algorithm. The ROC-AUC statistical comparison revealed that 
the Bi-LSTM model had an AUC of approximately 1.00. The Bi-LSTM model performed significantly better than 
the other models, with p-values less than 0.05.

Conclusion
The study presents a system for recognizing earthquake precursors. It utilizes animal vocal behavior analysis. 
We used ML and DL techniques to identify earthquakes effectively. The experimental results indicate that 
deep sequential models are more effective than traditional ML techniques. The Bi-LSTM model excelled all 
others, achieving a testing accuracy of 98.87%. It showed an AUC of 1.00, highlighting its strong generalization 
capability and robustness. GRU and LSTM models showed competitive results. Recurrent architectures are 
useful for time-series earthquake detection. Scalability analysis was conducted for the proposed system using 
3000 unseen audio samples, confirming the reliability of the system under increased workload conditions. It 
highlights the potency of the bioacoustics method, combined with the latest deep learning techniques, for the 
early detection of earthquakes. It has also identified the system as a cost-effective, supplementary early warning 
system for regions without the latest seismic facilities.
Future work
The future research directions include IoT-based acoustic sensors for real-time data collection and long-term 
environmental monitoring. Edge computing aims to reduce latency to facilitate faster decision-making in 
seismically active areas that are hard to reach. We aim to develop large-scale, sensor-based, and systematically 
annotated bioacoustics datasets collected from controlled environments and real-time monitoring systems 
in earthquake-prone regions. The addition of more data from different species of animals from different 
geographical locations is meant to improve the generalization capability of the model.
