earthquake frequency forecasting in indonesian regions
Wenwen Hou1

https://doi.org/10.1007/s11069-025-07744-9
Abstract
Despite the devastating impact of earthquakes, they offer potential for machine learn­
ing prediction to mitigate damage. This study explores the application of common al­
gorithms like Random Forests, Support Vector Machines (SVMs), XGBoost, and Long 
Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks alongside the Autoregressive Integrated Moving 
Average (ARIMA) framework for earthquake frequency forecasting in Indonesian regions. 
A novel hybrid model combining machine learning with ARIMA for multi-step forecasting 
is introduced. Surprisingly, the LSTM model, renowned for its strong predictive capability 
in nonlinear relationships, performed significantly lower than traditional machine learning 
methods based on metrics such as Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute 
Error (MAE). The results highlight the superior predictive capability of the hybrid ARI­
MA-XGBoost and ARIMA-RandomForest models in multi-step forecasting. These find­
ings underscore the continued relevance and effectiveness of traditional machine learning 
approaches in earthquake data prediction, suggesting avenues for future research to refine 
hybrid models and improve multi-step regression forecasting accuracy.
Keywords  ARIMA · Random forest · LSTM · Support vector regression · XGBoost
1  Introduction
Earthquakes pose a significant threat to human life, especially in urban areas within seis­
mic zones. Despite acknowledged challenges in accurately predicting earthquakes by the 
USGS, numerous scientists are dedicated to refining prediction models. Seismic time series 
data exhibits distinctive characteristics, but the infrequency of seismic events presents a 
formidable obstacle to achieving precise predictions, even in the era of advanced artificial 
intelligence. Moreover, limited data (Mignan and Broccardo 2020) availability significantly 
impairs prediction models’ performance (Carlson et  al. 1994), necessitating exploration

inundated with various statistical models. Such endeavors will contribute to enhancing 
our understanding of earthquake dynamics and improving urban resilience against seismic 
hazards (McGuire 2008; Probabilities 1995; Wang 2011). Furthermore, seismic research 
issues are diverse, with some scholars focusing on utilizing machine learning models for 
discriminating and classifying seismic events (Gulia and Wiemer 2019; Linville et al. 2019; 
Meier et al. 2019). Meanwhile, there is a growing expansion of machine learning techniques 
in seismology (Kong et al. 2019) provides a comprehensive overview of five prominent 
research domains within seismology where ML algorithms demonstrate significant poten­
tial: earthquake detection and phase selection, earthquake early warning (EEW) systems, 
ground motion prediction, seismic tomography, and seismic geodesy. Among these areas, 
earthquake early warning exhibits a relatively closer alignment with earthquake prediction 
research, indicating promising avenues for future exploration.
Reynen and Audet (2017) suggest utilizing supervised machine learning algorithms to 
discriminate between explosions and earthquakes, thereby mitigating potential erroneous 
reporting of seismic events by governmental authorities. They introduce a novel approach 
employing machine learning techniques for event classification and detection within seis­
mic networks. Asim et al. (2017) investigate the Hindu Kush region, leveraging time series 
data of seismic activity with machine learning classifiers to forecast earthquake magnitudes. 
Wrona et al. (2018) employ machine learning techniques for seismic profiling, demonstrat­
ing enhanced efficiency in seismic profiling. Ghosh et al. (2019) highlight the emergence 
of the support vector machine algorithm, augmenting its applicability in various domains 
where linear decision boundaries may be insufficient.
Although various prediction models are available for earthquakes, it’s important to note 
that these methods may not be suitable for short-term earthquake prediction as some precur­
sory signals are not specific to earthquakes. For instance, ionospheric disturbances caused 
by seismic activity may indicate the occurrence of natural phenomena such as solar storms 
(Astafyeva 2019). Additionally, certain features may differ depending on the geographic 
location or season (Arkan et al. 2023). Time series analysis serves to explore patterns and 
trends in data over time, enabling the prediction of future trends and revealing cyclical 
patterns. In seismology, time series analysis has been applied to study the time intervals 
between earthquakes (Gulia and Wiemer 2019; Li et al. 2022; Mousavi et al. 2020), where 
"natural time" emerged as one prevalent method for analyzing complex time series, particu­
larly seismic time series. Parameterizable time series models offer a re-parameterization of 
time suitable for investigating various geophysical systems (Bardossy and Duckstein 2022; 
Kvas et al. 2019; Mousavi et al. 2020). Presently, commonly used time series models include 
AR, MA, ARIMA models, among others (Ambrosino et al. 2020; Mandrikova et al. 2021). 
Predicting time series data is also a crucial area of research in seismic engineer area. In the 
context of time series data forecasting, the initial inclination gravitated towards employing 
time series models, such as AR (Autoregressive), MA (Moving Average), ARIMA (Autore­
gressive Integrated Moving Average), and analogous models (Amei et al. 2012; Gao et al. 
2021; Musarat et al. 2021). Furthermore, hybrid models that combine ARIMA with machine 
learning algorithms are gaining traction. Júnior et al. (2019) present a hybrid system to iden­
tify an optimal function for combining linear and nonlinear models’ predictions. Nguyen 
(2020) propose a novel approach that combines ARIMA and Statistical Machine Learning 
Models for water level forecasting. Ma et al. (2020) propose a method termed NN-ARIMA

2.2.2  Support vector regression
Support Vector Regression (SVR) differs substantially from most regression algorithms in 
that it focuses on minimizing errors (Al Banna et al. 2020) within a specific range, rather 
than attempting to minimize the sum of squared errors. Support Vector Regression (Aljarah 
et al. 2018) is a machine learning algorithm that utilizes the principles of Support Vec­
tor Machines (SVM) for regression tasks. It aims to predict continuous target variables by 
constructing a hyperplane that maximizes the margin around a subset of the training data, 
known as support vectors. Additionally, SVR employs kernel functions such as polynomial 
and Gaussian radial basis functions to address nonlinear relationships, thereby capturing 
complex patterns in the data and rendering it a robust tool for handling nonlinear regres­
sion problems. The algorithm seeks to find the optimal hyperplane that minimizes the error 
between the predicted values and the actual target values, while simultaneously maximizing 
the margin around the hyperplane (Ghosh et al. 2019).

2.2.7  Hybrid model
In this study, a hybrid model approach (Nguyen et al. 2019) is employed, combining the 
ARIMA model with SVM, Random Forest, and XGBOOST for prediction purposes. Based 
on this research, we construct a new hybrid model. The methodology involves several steps: 
Firstly, the ARIMA model is utilized to generate fitted predicted values. Next, the residu­
als, which represent the differences between the fitted and observed values, are computed. 
Subsequently, a machine learning model is constructed to establish a regression relationship 
between the residuals and the observed values. Finally, the future residuals are predicted, 
and the resulting predictions are transformed to obtain the actual frequency values. This 
integrated approach allows for a comprehensive and accurate prediction framework. Figure  
7 presents a schematic diagram illustrating the structural framework of the hybrid model.
In this study, we propose a hybrid modeling approach that integrates the Autoregres­
sive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model with advanced machine learning tech­
niques such as Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest (RF), and Extreme Gradient 
Boosting (XGBoost) for the purpose of time series prediction. This methodology exploits 
the linear prediction capabilities of traditional time series models and the nonlinear pattern 
recognition strengths of machine learning algorithms, thus enhancing the overall predictive 
accuracy.

ness in minimizing forecasting errors.
Furthermore, the high R-squared values of 0.98 for both ARIMA-Random Forest and 
ARIMA-XGBOOST affirm their robustness in capturing the variance in the data, highlight­
ing their superior performance in predicting earthquake frequency. Conversely, ARIMA-
SVR and ARIMA-LSTM exhibit comparatively higher RMSE and MAE values, implying 
less precise predictions. However, it’s worth noting that R-squared values are unavailable 
for ARIMA-LSTM, limiting a comprehensive comparison. Overall, the findings underscore 
the efficacy of ensemble methods such as Random Forest and XGBOOST in earthquake 
frequency forecasting, offering promising avenues for future research and practical applica­
tions in disaster management and mitigation strategies (Fig 9).
Figure 10 depicts the predictive performance of traditional machine learning models 
SVR, RF, and XGBoost in conjunction with ARIMA. It is evident that the forecasting accu­
racy of ARIMA-RF and ARIMA-XGBoost, based on tree regression and ensemble meth­
ods, closely matches the true values of the data.
4  Conclusion
In our multi-step forecasting, ARIMA-RF and ARIMA-XGBoost hybrid models outper­
form others significantly. Incorporating residuals enhances seismic frequency time series 
analysis, offering insights into earthquake prediction models. Surprisingly, LSTM model 
performance lags behind integrated models, challenging assumptions about deep learn­
ing superiority. Future research should explore models incorporating residual features for 
enhanced interpretability and predictive accuracy. Critically assessing model strengths and 
limitations is vital. Our study’s limitations include a small sample size, prompting inves­
tigation into longer and more accurate prediction models. Leveraging these insights can 
improve hybrid models and multi-step regression prediction accuracy.
